# SKILL: Responding to Peer Review (Academic Manuscripts)

## Purpose
Provide structured, professional, and effective responses to peer reviewers and editors in academic publishing.
Optimize for acceptance probability while preserving intellectual integrity.

---

## Core Principles

1. **Civility over correctness**
   - Always respond with a respectful, neutral tone.
   - Never sound defensive, sarcastic, or dismissive.

2. **Assume good faith**
   - Treat reviewer comments as legitimate contributions, even if flawed.

3. **Clarity is responsibility**
   - If a reviewer misunderstood, assume the text allowed misinterpretation.

4. **Selective agreement**
   - You are not required to accept all suggestions.
   - You must justify any rejection clearly and politely.

5. **Credibility through concessions**
   - Accept easy or valid changes to strengthen position when declining others.

---

## Response Structure (MANDATORY)

For each reviewer:

- Start a new numbered list.
- For each comment:

### Format:

**Reviewer Comment [#]:**
[Quote or accurately restate the reviewer's comment]

**Response:**
[Standalone answer — must be fully understandable without cross-reference]

---

## Writing Rules

- Each response must:
  - Stand on its own (no "see response above")
  - Address exactly one issue
  - Be concise but complete
  - Avoid emotional language

- Always:
  - Acknowledge the concern
  - Indicate action taken (or justify not taking it)
  - Specify manuscript changes when applicable

---

## Tone Constraints

### NEVER SAY:
- "The reviewer misunderstood"
- "This is incorrect"
- "This is obvious"
- "The reviewer is mistaken"
- "This is outside our interest" (without justification)

### ALWAYS PREFER:
- "We appreciate this comment"
- "We agree that…"
- "We have clarified…"
- "We respectfully note that…"
- "This is an interesting suggestion; however…"

---

## Tactical Patterns

### 1. When the reviewer misunderstood

**Use:**
> "We appreciate this observation. We recognize that the original wording may have been unclear. We have revised the manuscript to clarify that [correct interpretation]."

---

### 2. When accepting a suggestion

**Use:**
> "We agree that this is an important point. We have incorporated this suggestion and revised Section [X] accordingly."

---

### 3. When rejecting a suggestion (scope)

**Use:**
> "This is an interesting and valuable suggestion. However, we believe it falls outside the scope of the present study. To address this concern, we have clarified [relevant aspect] in the manuscript."

---

### 4. When reviewer asks for additional experiments

**Use:**
> "We agree that this analysis would provide additional insights. However, it would require substantial expansion beyond the current study's scope. We have acknowledged this as a limitation in Section [X]."

---

### 5. When criticism is about clarity

**Use:**
> "We appreciate this comment and agree that the text could be clearer. We have revised the relevant section to improve clarity and precision."

---

### 6. When reviewer challenges conclusions

**Use:**
> "We agree that our interpretation should be presented cautiously. We have revised the manuscript to clarify that our conclusions are supported by the data but remain interpretative."

---

### 7. When reviewer points out an error

**Use:**
> "We thank the reviewer for identifying this issue. We have corrected the error and updated the manuscript accordingly."

---

### 8. When no clear answer exists

**Use:**
> "This is an important question. To our knowledge, there is currently no definitive answer in the literature. We have clarified this limitation in the manuscript."

---

## Strategic Heuristics

- **If confusion exists → clarify text**
- **If suggestion is easy → accept**
- **If suggestion is costly → acknowledge + scope limitation**
- **If reviewer is wrong → fix ambiguity, not reviewer**
- **If rejecting → always concede partial validity**

---

## Red Flags (AVOID)

- Confrontational tone
- Comparing reviewers
- Ignoring a comment
- Vague responses ("we addressed this" without specifics)
- Over-defending instead of clarifying

## Meta-Goal

Maximize:
- Perceived professionalism
- Editorial trust
- Reviewer satisfaction

Minimize:
- Friction
- Ambiguity
- Ego signaling

---

## Behavioral Directive

When generating responses:
- Default to diplomacy over precision conflict
- Convert disagreement into clarification
- Preserve author authority without confrontation
