# FRAT Fatigue: Why Manual Risk Assessment Fails on Multi-Sector Days

**By CrewBrief Operations** · May 2026 · 6 min read

---

The first sector of the day, the FRAT gets attention. The pilot reads each question, considers the answer, and provides a thoughtful assessment.

By the third sector, the same FRAT — identical questions, identical format, identical scale — is an exercise in muscle memory. Clicks without cognition. The tool designed to catch risk becomes a checkbox ritual.

This is FRAT fatigue, and it is the single biggest threat to the effectiveness of manual risk assessment programs.

## The Problem with Repetition

Human attention is a finite resource. When a task is repeated multiple times in a single day, the brain optimizes for efficiency — and what gets optimized out is the careful consideration that the task was designed to capture.

Research on electronic checklist behavior in aviation shows that compliance and accuracy decline with repetition. The first iteration of a 25-item checklist averages 98% accuracy. By the fifth iteration, accuracy can fall below 80%.

FRATs are particularly vulnerable because:

- **The inputs are mostly the same.** Weather, duty time, and crew pairing rarely change dramatically between consecutive sectors. The pilot is asked to re-enter data that hasn't changed, which conditions them to click through without thinking.
- **The stakes feel lower.** After the first successful takeoff, the perceived risk of subsequent sectors decreases — even when objective risk factors (fatigue, deteriorating weather) are increasing.
- **The format is repetitive.** A 25-question form, identical every time, invites pattern-matching rather than genuine assessment.

## How Automation Changes the Equation

An automated FRAT eliminates the cognitive overhead of data re-entry. When the system pre-fills 90–95% of risk factors from available operational data, the pilot's task shifts from "fill out this form" to "confirm or adjust this assessment."

The difference is meaningful:

| Aspect | Manual FRAT | Automated FRAT |
|--------|-------------|----------------|
| Time to complete | 3–4 minutes | 30–45 seconds |
| Questions requiring judgment | 25 | 1–3 |
| Data re-entry required | Yes (weather, duty, route) | No (pre-filled) |
| Risk of fatigue-driven skipping | High | Low |
| Audit trail quality | Variable | Complete |

The third sector FRAT becomes: scan the pre-filled scores, confirm the fatigue self-assessment, adjust if needed, and move on. The entire interaction takes less than a minute and preserves the cognitive space for the judgment that matters.

## The Transparency Principle

For automated FRATs to work on multi-sector days, the scoring must be transparent. A pilot on the fourth sector of a 12-hour duty day does not have the mental bandwidth to reverse-engineer a black-box risk score.

Every factor's contribution must be visible and explorable:

- Weather risk: 4 points (crosswind 18 kts, threshold: 15 kts)
- Fatigue risk: 6 points (duty day 11h, threshold: 8h) — *pilot notes: slept poorly*
- Airport familiarity: 1 point (visited 8 times)
- Total: 11 points — MODERATE RISK

The pilot can inspect any factor, see the raw data, and override if their situational awareness suggests a different assessment. The override is logged — creating an audit trail for the SMS program without creating friction for the crew.

## Preventing Automation Complacency

The legitimate concern with automation is complacency: if the system scores risk, won't the pilot simply accept the score without thinking?

The evidence from CrewBrief's beta suggests the opposite. When the automated FRAT is transparent and quick, pilots engage more, not less — because the 30 seconds they spend confirming the pre-filled assessment is 30 seconds of focused judgment, not 3 minutes of data entry.

The key metrics to monitor:
- **Override rate.** A healthy 5–15% indicates active engagement. Below 5% may indicate complacency. Above 15% may indicate the automation is scoring incorrectly.
- **Time to complete.** If times creep up over the day, engagement is flagging. If they stay consistent, the automation is doing its job.
- **Correlation with outcomes.** Do automated FRAT scores correlate with safety events? This is the ultimate validation.

## The Bottom Line for Multi-Sector Operators

If your operation runs multiple sectors per day — charter, regional, air taxi — your FRAT program is only as good as the third sector. A manual FRAT that takes 3 minutes on sector 1 will be rushed in 45 seconds on sector 4, with corresponding quality loss.

An automated FRAT that takes 30 seconds every time, with pre-filled data from operational sources, delivers the same quality on the last sector as the first. That consistency is the goal — and it is achievable with the right automation architecture.
