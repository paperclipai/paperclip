# FAA Crew Briefing Requirements: Compliance Guide for Modern Operators

**By CrewBrief Operations** · May 2026 · 7 min read

---

Few questions generate as much confusion in flight operations as "what does the FAA actually require in a crew briefing?" The answer depends on the operating rule, the type of operation, and — increasingly — the operator's Safety Management System.

This guide covers the regulatory foundation for crew briefings under Part 91 and Part 135, what those requirements mean in practice, and how automation can help operators meet and exceed compliance obligations.

## The Regulatory Foundation

### Part 91 — General Operating and Flight Rules

Part 91 does not prescribe a specific briefing format or checklist. Instead, it establishes the pilot-in-command's responsibility to become familiar with all available information concerning the flight:

**§ 91.103 — Preflight Action.** "Each pilot in command shall, before beginning a flight, become familiar with all available information concerning that flight. This information must include:

- (a) Weather reports and forecasts
- (b) Fuel requirements
- (c) Alternatives available if the planned flight cannot be completed
- (d) Any known traffic delays of which the pilot in command has been advised by ATC
- (e) Runway lengths at airports of intended use
- (f) The following additional information for IFR flights: weather reports and forecasts, fuel requirements, alternatives available, and any known traffic delays"

The key phrase is "all available information." The regulation does not specify a minimum set of sources, a format, or a retention period. But in practice, the PIC's preflight action must be demonstrable. If an incident occurs and the question arises "was the crew adequately briefed?", the operator needs to show what information was available, when it was reviewed, and by whom.

### Part 135 — Commuter and On-Demand Operations

Part 135 imposes more specific requirements, particularly for flight crewmember duties:

**§ 135.63 — Recordkeeping.** The certificate holder must maintain records of each crewmember's duty and rest periods. While this does not directly mandate briefing records, it establishes the expectation that crew activity — including briefing — is documented.

**§ 135.65 — Briefing of passengers before takeoff.** This covers passenger safety briefings (seat belts, emergency exits, etc.), not the operational crew briefing we discuss here.

**§ 135.100 — Flight Locator.** The certificate holder must have a system for locating each flight. An automated briefing system that timestamps crew acknowledgment provides a natural flight-location data point.

The operational briefing requirements for Part 135 are largely derived from the certificate holder's approved manual (135.23 — Manual contents) and the PIC's preflight responsibilities. Most Part 135 operations manuals include specific briefing procedures that have been accepted by the FAA as part of the certificate holder's approved programs.

### The Growing Role of SMS

While Part 5 (SMS requirements for Part 121 operators) does not directly apply to most Part 91 and 135 operators, the FAA has signaled its intent to expand SMS requirements. The SMS voluntary program for Part 135 operators, paired with insurance industry trends, means that formal risk assessment as part of crew briefing is becoming a de facto standard.

An SMS-compliant briefing process typically includes:

- **Hazard identification** — weather, airspace, airport, and operational hazards identified before each flight
- **Risk assessment** — each hazard evaluated for severity and likelihood
- **Mitigation** — controls identified for hazards above the operator's risk tolerance
- **Documentation** — the assessment recorded and retained for review

## What a Compliant Briefing Looks Like

Based on the regulatory framework and SMS best practices, a defensible crew briefing should include:

### Pre-Flight Information

1. **Current and forecast weather** for departure, destination, and alternates — including METARs, TAFs, SIGMETs, AIRMETs, and winds aloft
2. **NOTAMs** relevant to the route, airports, and airspace — including FDC NOTAMs, airport closures, nav-aid outages, and airspace restrictions
3. **Fuel planning data** — burn calculations, reserves, alternate fuel requirements
4. **Route information** — filed flight plan, altitude, expected routing, and any restrictions
5. **Aircraft status** — MEL items, deferred maintenance, configuration limitations
6. **Crew status** — duty time, rest periods, fatigue assessment

### Risk Assessment

A structured evaluation of the factors above, with:
- Identified hazards and their severity
- Risk scores or ratings
- Mitigation measures for risks above acceptable thresholds
- A determination of whether the flight can proceed

### Documentation

Records that show:
- What information was available to the crew before the flight
- When the briefing was reviewed
- Who reviewed it
- What risk assessment was performed
- Any changes that occurred between the initial briefing and departure

## How Automation Supports Compliance

### Comprehensive Data Assembly

Automated briefing tools ingest from authoritative FAA sources — weather feeds, NOTAM databases, and airspace data — and assemble everything into a single document. This ensures that the "all available information" standard under 91.103 is met consistently, without relying on the crew to remember every source.

### Consistent Process

A manual briefing process varies from crew member to crew member. One pilot checks all six sources thoroughly; another skips the winds aloft because they're in a hurry. Automated assembly ensures every briefing includes the same categories of information, every time. Consistency is the foundation of a defensible process.

### Built-in Risk Assessment

Integrating risk assessment into the briefing workflow ensures that hazards are identified and evaluated before every flight, not just when the crew has extra time. An automated FRAT with pre-filled risk factors removes the burden of manual data entry and ensures the assessment is complete.

### Audit Trail

Every briefing delivery, acknowledgment, and delta review is timestamped and associated with a specific crew member. When the FAA asks for records, the operator can produce a complete history: what was briefed, when, and by whom.

This audit trail is valuable beyond compliance. It supports internal safety investigations, insurance claims, and continuous improvement programs. When an incident occurs, the question shifts from "was there a briefing?" to "what did the briefing contain, and what did the crew do with it?"

### Change Management

Weather and airspace conditions change between the briefing and departure. An automated system tracks those changes, highlights them for the crew, and retains the version history. This addresses a common compliance gap: the discrepancy between the conditions at brief time and the conditions at departure time.

## Common Compliance Gaps

Operators reviewed by the FAA or in SMS audits frequently show these gaps:

| Gap | Typical Cause | Automated Solution |
|-----|--------------|-------------------|
| Inconsistent data sources | Crew-dependent manual gathering | Automated ingestion from authoritative sources |
| Missing risk assessment | FRAT seen as optional or time-consuming | Embedded FRAT with pre-filled factors |
| No documentation of changes | No system to track delta between brief and departure | Version history with delta markers |
| Incomplete audit trail | No records of what was briefed or when | Time-stamped delivery and acknowledgment records |
| Crew fatigue not assessed | Fatigue treated as self-reported only | Objective fatigue scoring from duty/rest data |

## Preparing for SMS Expansion

Whether SMS becomes mandatory for Part 135 operators through regulation or de facto through insurance requirements, the operators who already have structured, documented briefing processes will be ahead. The elements that SMS auditors look for — hazard identification, risk assessment, mitigation, documentation, and continuous improvement — map directly onto the capabilities of a well-designed automated briefing system.

Operators should treat crew briefing not as a regulatory checkbox but as the operational control point it is. The briefing is where data becomes a decision. Automating the data assembly and risk assessment steps frees the crew to focus on the decision itself.
